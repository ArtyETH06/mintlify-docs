---
title: "How Launches Work"
description: "A launch has three main parts: setup, ticket sale, and settlement. Each part is on-chain, driven by a few parameters instead of ad-hoc decisions."
---

## 1. Setup: Rules, Not Hype

The founder sets:

- Team allocation (you set this; the system derives the rest).
- Sale supply and vesting bands are auto-calculated from the team share and applied via Streamflow.
- Ticket price, max tickets per wallet, and min/max caps in SOL.
- Optional early phase buyback (taking a portion of the LP fees to buyback some token and add it into the LP position, locked).
- Optional compounding (taking a portion of the LP fees to add more liquidity reserve).
- External identity proofs so users see who owns the launch, not just the wallet.

<Warning>
  Once deployed, these rules cannot be changed quietly; the smart contract enforces them for the whole launch.
</Warning>

## 2. Ticket Sale: One Simple Action

Participants buy tickets from the launch contract using SOL.

- Each ticket is a claim on tokens if the sale succeeds.
- Per-wallet limits are not a silver bullet; they help cap low‑effort bundling, and, combined with our wallet scoring and profiling, let us flag risky, wallet‑concentrated launches as tickets come in.

The sale runs until a defined end time.

## 3. Settlement: Three Clear Outcomes

At the end, the contract checks how much SOL was raised and applies a simple decision tree:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Below min cap">
    Everyone can claim a **refund** in SOL; no tokens are distributed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Between min and max cap">
    All valid tickets receive tokens **pro-rata**.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Above max cap (oversubscribed)">
    The extra demand triggers a **raffle and anti-whale logic**, so oversized entries are trimmed first.
  </Step>
</Steps>

No one runs a manual draw; the contract resolves everything deterministically from on-chain data.
